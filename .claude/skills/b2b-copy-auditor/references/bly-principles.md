## 1. The 4 U's Formula

Every headline, subhead, and CTA should be evaluated against four criteria.
Score each from 1 (weak) to 4 (strong):

- **Urgent** — Does it give the reader a reason to act now? Is there a time element
  or consequence of delay? For property managers: seasonal leasing cycles, rising
  costs, portfolio deadlines.
- **Unique** — Is this a claim only your company can make, or could any competitor
  paste their logo on it? Generic consulting language ("we help you grow") fails here.
- **Ultra-specific** — Does it use concrete numbers, timeframes, or outcomes instead
  of vague promises? "Reduce vacancy by 3 units per quarter" beats "reduce vacancy."
- **Useful** — Does it promise a clear benefit the reader actually wants? For the
  target buyer: margin improvement, portfolio growth without new headcount, vendor
  accountability.

**Audit application:** Score every headline and CTA on the page using 4 U's. Any
element scoring below 3 on two or more U's needs a rewrite.

## 2. The BDF (Beliefs, Desires, Feelings) Framework

Before writing or auditing, identify the prospect's core buying complex:

- **Beliefs** — What does the prospect believe about their situation and about
  consulting firms? Property managers in B/C class often believe: consultants don't
  understand their margins; technology is oversold; their business is fundamentally
  about people, not systems; outside firms can't grasp the day-to-day grind.
- **Desires** — What do they want? Portfolio expansion without proportional headcount
  growth. Better NOI. Reliable vendor relationships. Operational playbooks that
  actually get used. Less firefighting.
- **Feelings** — What emotions drive decisions? Frustration with false promises.
  Pride in running lean. Fear of overextending. Skepticism toward anything that
  sounds too polished. Relief when someone "gets it."

**Audit application:** For every page, check whether the copy acknowledges at least
one Belief, speaks to at least one Desire, and triggers at least one Feeling. Copy
that only lists services without touching BDF will not convert this audience.

## 3. The Motivating Sequence (5 Steps)

Bly's adaptation of the classic persuasion arc. Every conversion-oriented page
should follow this flow, even if compressed:

1. **Get attention** — Headline must interrupt the reader's pattern. For skeptical
   B2B buyers, this often means naming their specific problem rather than claiming
   your solution.
2. **Show the need** — Describe the problem or opportunity. Agitate it. Make the
   reader feel the cost of inaction. For property managers: what happens to margins
   when you scale without systems? What does turnover cost when your on-site teams
   are undertrained?
3. **Satisfy the need** — Present your solution as the logical answer to the problem
   you just made vivid. This is where your services appear — but framed as the
   relief, not the pitch.
4. **Prove your claims** — Testimonials, case studies, specific results, client
   logos, third-party validation. Bly calls this "The Power of Proof" and considers
   it the single most underused element in B2B copy.
5. **Ask for action** — Clear, specific CTA. Not "learn more" but "schedule a
   15-minute portfolio review" or "get your operations scorecard."

**Audit application:** Map each page's sections to these five steps. Identify which
steps are missing or weak. Most B2B sites skip steps 2 and 4 entirely.

## 4. The FAB Pyramid (Four Tiers)

Organize product/service information in four layers:

- **Features** (base) — What the service includes. Operational audits, staffing
  models, vendor procurement, training programs.
- **Advantages** (second tier) — How that feature works compared to alternatives.
  "Unlike general consultants, we've managed B/C portfolios ourselves."
- **Benefits** (third tier) — What the advantage means for the reader's business.
  "You keep your lean team and still add 200 units this year."
- **Ultimate Benefits** (top) — The highest-order personal or business outcome.
  The thing the operator lies awake thinking about. "Sleep at night knowing your
  portfolio can weather a downturn without layoffs." Ultimate benefits connect to
  identity, security, pride, or long-term survival — not just operational metrics.

**Audit application:** Check that every feature mentioned on the page is carried
through to at least the Advantage level. The Benefit level should appear in
headlines, subheads, and CTAs. Headlines and hero copy should reach for Ultimate
Benefits where possible — these are the lines that make a skeptical operator stop
scrolling. Features alone are not persuasive.

## 5. Eight Headline Types That Work

From The Copywriter's Handbook. Use these as audit templates:

1. **Direct** — States the benefit outright. "Cut your per-unit operating cost by 18%."
2. **Indirect** — Provokes curiosity. "What 200-unit operators know that 50-unit
   operators don't."
3. **News** — Announces something. "A new model for B/C portfolio growth without
   new overhead."
4. **How-to** — Promises instruction. "How to expand your portfolio without expanding
   your payroll."
5. **Question** — Engages by asking. "Are your on-site teams costing you more than
   your vacancies?"
6. **Command** — Directs action. "Stop losing money on vendor contracts you didn't
   negotiate."
7. **Reason-why** — Lists benefits. "7 reasons your next 100 units won't need 10
   new hires."
8. **Testimonial** — Uses a client's words. "They helped us go from 340 to 800
   units in 18 months without adding management overhead."

**Audit application:** Classify each headline on the page. If a headline doesn't
fit any of these eight types, it's likely too vague or too clever to convert.

## 6. The 4 C's Copywriting Formula

Bly's modern framework for evaluating all B2B copy:

- **Clear** — Can a first-time visitor understand what you do and who you do it for
  within 5 seconds?
- **Concise** — Is every sentence earning its place? Are there filler phrases,
  redundancies, or corporate padding?
- **Compelling** — Does the copy make the reader want to keep reading? Is there a
  reason to care?
- **Credible** — Does the copy back its claims with proof? Or does it rely on
  self-praise and superlatives?

**Audit application:** Rate every major text block (hero, features section, about
section, CTA area) on each C, 1-4 scale. Blocks scoring below 3 on any C need work.

## 7. The Power of Proof

Bly considers proof the most powerful and most neglected element in B2B copy. Types
of proof, ranked by persuasive power for skeptical B2B buyers:

1. **Specific results with numbers** — "Reduced turnover 22% across 14 properties
   in the first year." Strongest form.
2. **Case studies** — Named client, named problem, named outcome.
3. **Testimonials with attribution** — Name, title, company. Anonymous testimonials
   are nearly worthless to this audience.
4. **Client logos / portfolio size** — Shows scale without requiring the reader to
   trust your words.
5. **Methodology description** — Showing how you work (not just what you deliver)
   builds credibility with operators who value process.
6. **Credentials and tenure** — Years in business, certifications, associations.
   Weaker than results but still useful.
7. **Guarantees and risk reversal** — Especially powerful for an audience burned by
   vendor promises.

**Audit application:** Count proof elements on each page. Any page with fewer than
three proof elements is underperforming. Service pages and the homepage should have
at least five.

## 8. B2B Emotion — The Hidden Driver

From Bly's chapter "Are Businesspeople Devoid of Emotion When Making Buying
Decisions?" — the answer is no. B2B buyers are humans. They experience:

- Fear of making a bad decision (especially with tight budgets)
- Frustration with current pain points
- Pride in running a lean, effective operation
- Hope that there's a better way
- Relief when someone demonstrates real understanding

**Audit application:** Check whether the copy addresses emotional drivers or only
logical ones. Copy that is purely rational (features + pricing) will underperform
copy that acknowledges the reader's frustration and offers emotional relief before
presenting logical proof.

## 9. Specificity as Credibility

Vague claims trigger skepticism. Specific claims build trust. Examples:

| Vague (fails) | Specific (converts) |
|---|---|
| "We've helped many clients" | "We've supported 47 operators across 12 states" |
| "Significant cost savings" | "Average 14% reduction in per-unit operating cost" |
| "Years of experience" | "22 years managing B and C class portfolios" |
| "Improved occupancy" | "Moved occupancy from 88% to 96% in 9 months" |

**Audit application:** Highlight every vague claim on the page. Each one needs
either a specific number or a specific story to replace it.

## 10. Jargon Rules for B2B

Bly's position: use jargon when writing to an audience that uses that jargon.
Property managers know and expect terms like NOI, cap rate, per-unit cost, turn
cost, lease-up, Class B/C, value-add, stabilized occupancy. Using these terms
signals insider knowledge. Avoiding them signals outsider status.

However: avoid consultant jargon that the operator audience does not use. Terms
like "synergy," "leverage," "holistic approach," "stakeholder alignment,"
"paradigm" — these trigger the exact vendor-BS alarm that this audience is
primed to detect.

**Audit application:** Flag any consultant/marketing jargon on the page. Flag
any place where industry-specific language should be used but isn't.

## 11. The "You" Orientation

Count the ratio of "you/your" to "we/our/I" on each page. Bly's rule: the
reader should be the hero, not the company. Copy that talks mostly about itself
("We are... We provide... Our team...") fails to connect.

**Audit application:** Compute the you:we ratio for each page. Target is at least
2:1 in favor of "you." Below 1:1 requires a rewrite.

## 12. Long Copy Doctrine

Bly's principle: "The more you tell, the more you sell." This does not mean
padding. It means: if you have a complex, high-consideration service (consulting
engagements are exactly this), your pages need enough copy to address objections,
present proof, and move the reader through the full Motivating Sequence.

A homepage with only a tagline and three icon boxes is almost certainly
underperforming for a consulting firm selling to skeptical buyers.

**Audit application:** Check whether each page has enough copy to complete the
Motivating Sequence. Thin pages (under 400 words on a service page) are
red-flagged for expansion.

## 13. Services Marketing Rules

From Bly's "Tactics and Tips for Marketing B2B Services":

- Services are intangible — you must make them concrete through process
  descriptions, deliverables lists, and outcome stories.
- The prospect is buying the people, not the service — bios, photos, and team
  credibility matter more than service descriptions.
- Risk is higher with services than products — guarantees, pilot programs, and
  "start small" offers reduce friction.
- Decision-makers buying services need to justify the spend internally — give them
  the language and data to do so.

**Audit application:** Does the page make the service tangible? Does it showcase
the team? Does it reduce perceived risk? Does it arm the buyer with internal
justification language?

## 14. Notional Value Anchoring

When offering a free or low-cost entry point (diagnostic call, portfolio review,
scorecard), state its notional market value explicitly: "A Portfolio Margin Review
that would normally cost $3,500 — yours at no charge."

This works for three reasons:

1. **Specificity as credibility** — a precise dollar figure signals real substance,
   not a throwaway freebie.
2. **Risk reversal amplification** — "no cost" is good, but "normally $3,500, no
   cost to you" makes the risk calculus unmissable.
3. **Internal justification** — the buyer who needs to explain the engagement to a
   partner or board can point to concrete value received before a dollar was spent.

**Audit application:** Check whether free offers and lead magnets on the page
include a value anchor. If the CTA is "Schedule a free consultation," it should
be rewritten to name the deliverable and anchor its value.
