## 1. The 3-Part Conversion Copywriting Process

Wiebe's process is non-negotiable. No step is optional:

### Part 1: Research and Discovery
- The largest portion of the work. If it isn't, you're doing it wrong.
- Sources: customer surveys, interviews, support tickets, sales call transcripts,
  online reviews of competitors, forums where the audience talks.
- Output: raw voice-of-customer data organized by message category.
- For property management consulting: mine BiggerPockets forums, NAA conference
  discussions, property management Facebook groups, Reddit r/propertymanagement,
  reviews of competing consultants and proptech vendors.

### Part 2: Writing, Wireframing, and Editing
- Split screen method: research on one side, writing document on the other.
- Copy is assembled from VOC data, not invented from scratch.
- Apply templates and frameworks (PAS, AIDA, etc.) but fill them with customer
  language, not marketing language.
- Edit using the Seven Sweeps (see section 3).

### Part 3: Validation and Experimentation
- Five-second tests for clarity.
- Click tracking and scroll mapping for engagement.
- A/B testing where traffic supports it.
- Usertesting.com or equivalent for qualitative feedback.
- Copy is never "done" — it's the current best hypothesis.

**Audit application:** When auditing existing copy, you are essentially performing
a reverse-engineering of this process. You're checking whether the copy on the page
shows evidence of customer research (VOC language), proper structure (frameworks),
and clarity (editing sweeps). Copy that reads like marketing-department output rather
than customer-informed messaging fails this test.

## 2. Voice-of-Customer (VOC) Message Mining

The core technique that separates conversion copywriting from traditional copywriting.

### The 4 Key Questions (from Jennifer Havice / Wiebe methodology)

When surveying or interviewing prospects and customers, these four questions
surface the raw material for copy:

1. **"What was going on in your life/business that brought you to look for
   [this type of solution]?"**
   — Surfaces the triggering event. For property managers: "We'd just acquired
   our third property and realized our systems couldn't scale."

2. **"What almost kept you from buying/signing up?"**
   — Surfaces objections and anxieties. "I'd been burned by two consultants
   before who didn't understand B/C class."

3. **"What was the #1 thing that convinced you to go ahead?"**
   — Surfaces the decisive benefit. "They'd actually managed properties like
   ours — not just theorized about it."

4. **"What's the #1 benefit you've experienced since?"**
   — Surfaces the proof language. "We added 180 units in a year without hiring
   a single new regional manager."

### Message Mining Process

1. Export all survey/interview responses into a spreadsheet or document.
2. Read every response. Don't skim.
3. Copy-paste phrases that are:
   - **Emotionally charged** — frustration, relief, hope, fear
   - **Specific** — numbers, timeframes, named outcomes
   - **Recurring** — themes that appear across multiple respondents
   - **Surprising** — insights you wouldn't have written yourself
4. Categorize into columns:
   - Desired outcomes (what they want)
   - Pain points (what they're struggling with)
   - Hesitations/anxieties (what almost stopped them)
   - Decisive factors (what tipped the decision)
5. Rank by frequency. The most common themes form the messaging hierarchy.
6. Star any phrase that is particularly well-said — these may go into copy
   nearly verbatim.

### The Verbatim Principle

Wiebe's signature technique: the best copy is often pulled directly from what
customers say, lightly edited for flow. The famous example: testing "If you think
you need rehab, you do" (pulled from an Amazon book review) against the agency's
original "Your addiction ends here." The verbatim headline generated 400% more
CTA clicks and 20% more form submissions.

**Audit application:** Does the existing copy sound like something a customer would
say, or something a marketing department would write? Read each headline aloud.
If it sounds like a brochure, it needs VOC injection. If it sounds like a
frustrated property manager talking to a peer, it's on the right track.

## 3. The Seven Sweeps

Wiebe's editing framework. Each sweep is a single pass through the copy, looking
for one specific issue. Never combine sweeps — doing so creates blind spots.

### Sweep 1: Clarity
- The most important sweep. Always first.
- Can a cold reader understand what this page is about in 5 seconds?
- Watch for "and" clauses that dilute the message. "We help you reduce costs,
  improve operations, expand your portfolio, and build better teams" says
  everything and therefore says nothing. Pick one.
- Remove all ambiguity. "Better results" means nothing. "96% occupancy within
  6 months" means something.
- Check for the "curse of knowledge" — are you assuming the reader knows things
  they don't?

### Sweep 2: Voice and Tone
- Does the copy sound like the brand? Is it consistent page to page?
- For property management consulting: the tone should be competent, direct,
  experienced, and grounded. Not corporate-slick. Not startup-casual. Think
  "the smartest operator in the room who's been through it."
- Voice = who you are (consistent). Tone = your mood for this context (varies).

### Sweep 3: "So What?" Sweep
- After every claim, ask "so what?" on behalf of the reader.
- "We have 20 years of experience." So what?
- "We have 20 years of experience managing B and C class portfolios through
  two recessions." Now you're getting somewhere.
- Every passage must answer why the reader should care.

### Sweep 4: "Prove It" Sweep
- After every claim, ask "prove it."
- You cannot just state that you deliver results. You must demonstrate it.
- Pair with the "So What?" sweep: every claim should pass both tests.
- Sources of proof: testimonials, case studies, screenshots, data, client
  logos, methodology walkthroughs, guarantees.

### Sweep 5: Specificity Sweep
- Replace every vague word with a specific one.
- "Many" → exact number. "Significant" → percentage. "Experienced" → years
  and context. "Improved" → by how much, measured how.
- Specificity is the fastest way to build credibility with skeptical buyers.

### Sweep 6: Heightened Emotion Sweep
- Is the copy triggering the right emotions at the right moments?
- Opening: frustration or recognition ("you've been here before").
- Middle: hope or curiosity ("there's a way to do this without burning out
  your team").
- Close: confidence and urgency ("you can start this quarter").
- Emotion is not hype. Emotion is connection. This audience will reject hype
  but respond to empathy.

### Sweep 7: Zero Risk Sweep
- What is the reader risking by taking the next step?
- Remove every barrier: "No commitment," "15-minute call," "We'll send you
  a scorecard — no sales pitch."
- Address the implicit risk: "You won't be locked into a long engagement.
  We start with a 30-day diagnostic."
- For an audience burned by vendors: risk reversal is not optional. It is
  the difference between a click and a bounce.

**Audit application:** Run each sweep as a separate pass when auditing a page.
Document findings per sweep. This prevents the common mistake of trying to fix
everything at once, which leads to unfocused rewrites.

## 4. The Messaging Hierarchy

The order in which messages appear matters as much as the messages themselves.

### Building the Hierarchy

1. From VOC research, identify the top 3-4 messages by frequency and intensity.
2. The #1 message becomes the headline / hero area.
3. Messages #2-4 become section headers or supporting blocks.
4. Less frequent but still important messages become proof points, FAQ answers,
   or footer content.

### For Multifamily Consulting Context

Likely hierarchy based on the target audience profile:

1. **"We understand your actual business"** — Differentiator against generic
   consultants. Must appear in the hero.
2. **"You can grow without proportional overhead"** — The core desire. Must
   appear in the first scroll.
3. **"Here's proof it works"** — Case studies, numbers. Must appear before
   the CTA.
4. **"We won't waste your time or money"** — Risk reversal. Must appear
   near the CTA.

**Audit application:** Map the existing page's information architecture against
the likely messaging hierarchy. Are the most important messages in the most
prominent positions? Or is the hero occupied by a vague tagline while the real
value is buried in a paragraph at the bottom?

## 5. Copy-Data Gap Analysis

Wiebe's technique for finding what's missing on a page:

### Process

1. Pull out the key messages currently on the page. What does each section
   communicate?
2. Compare against VOC data. What do customers say matters most?
3. Identify gaps: messages that customers care about but the page doesn't
   address.
4. Identify mismatches: messages the page emphasizes that customers don't
   care about.
5. Identify ordering issues: messages in the wrong sequence.

### Common Gaps in B2B Consulting Sites

- **No acknowledgment of past bad experiences** — This audience has been
  burned. If you don't acknowledge it, they assume you'll do the same.
- **No process transparency** — "What actually happens when I hire you?"
  is unanswered on most consulting sites.
- **No evidence of industry-specific knowledge** — Generic business
  consulting language where property management language should be.
- **No internal justification** — The buyer needs to explain this spend
  to a partner or board. Give them the language.

**Audit application:** Perform the gap analysis for each page. Document what's
present, what's missing, and what's misplaced.

## 6. PAS (Problem-Agitation-Solution) for B2B

Wiebe emphasizes that the Agitation step is the most powerful — and the most
often skipped:

- **Problem:** Name the situation. "You're managing 300+ units with the same
  team you had at 150."
- **Agitation:** Make the reader feel the cost. "Every month that passes, your
  best site managers get closer to burnout. Your maintenance costs creep up
  because nobody has time to negotiate vendor contracts properly. And the
  property you just acquired? It's dragging down the NOI of your entire
  portfolio because the transition playbook doesn't exist."
- **Solution:** Present the relief. "We build the operational infrastructure
  that lets you scale — without scaling your headcount."

**Audit application:** Check whether service pages and the homepage use
agitation or jump straight from problem-naming to solution-pitching. Skipping
agitation is the #1 reason B2B service pages feel flat.

## 7. Wireframing and Information Architecture

Copy and layout are inseparable. When auditing, consider:

- **Above the fold:** Does it answer three questions: What is this? Who is it
  for? Why should I care?
- **First scroll:** Does it deepen the value proposition or just repeat the
  hero in different words?
- **Mid-page:** Are proof elements (testimonials, case studies, logos)
  positioned where skepticism peaks — just before the CTA?
- **CTA placement:** Is there a clear next step visible without excessive
  scrolling? Is there a secondary CTA for people not ready to commit?

## 8. Validation Before Launch

Methods for testing copy before (or after) it goes live:

- **Five-second test:** Show the page for 5 seconds. Ask: "What is this
  company? What do they do? Who is it for?" If answers are wrong or blank,
  clarity has failed.
- **Highlighter test:** Have 5-10 prospects read the page and highlight what
  resonates (green) and what doesn't (red). Patterns emerge fast.
- **First-click test:** Where do people click first? If it's not your primary
  CTA, your visual hierarchy needs work.

## 9. Conversion Copy vs. Brand Copy

Wiebe draws a hard line: conversion copy exists to move the reader toward a
specific action. Brand copy exists to build awareness and affinity.

Both have value. But a B2B consulting website needs conversion copy on its
service pages, homepage, and contact page. Brand copy belongs in blog posts,
about pages, and social content.

**Audit application:** Is the homepage trying to convert or trying to impress?
For this audience, conversion wins. They're not browsing — they're evaluating.
The page should feel like a conversation with a competent peer, not a brand
campaign.

## 11. The "Even If" Objection Pre-emption Technique

Attach an "even if" clause to benefit statements to neutralize the reader's
most likely objection in the same sentence. This prevents the skeptical buyer
from mentally checking out before reaching the proof.

Structure: **[Benefit] even if [objection you know they're thinking].**

Examples for multifamily consulting:

- "You can grow your portfolio 20-30% even if you can't justify another
  full-time regional."
- "Reduce labor hours per unit even if your teams are already running lean."
- "Standardize operations across properties even if every site manager does
  things differently."

The technique works because it shows you understand their specific constraints
— not generic objections, but the exact hesitations a B/C class operator
carries. It also forces the copywriter to name the objection, which builds
trust with an audience primed to detect hand-waving.

**Audit application:** Check whether benefit statements on the page address
likely objections inline or leave them unspoken. Any headline or key benefit
line that ignores the reader's probable "but..." is a candidate for an
"even if" rewrite.

## 12. Front-Loading

Put the most important, reader-relevant words at the start of headlines,
subheads, bullet points, and sentences. The reader's eye hits the left margin
first. If the first two or three words aren't about their problem, benefit,
or outcome, they may never reach the rest.

| Buried (weak) | Front-loaded (strong) |
|---|---|
| "Our team helps you reduce per-unit costs" | "Per-unit costs drop when..." |
| "With our consulting approach, you get..." | "You get operational playbooks that..." |
| "By working with us, your portfolio can..." | "Your portfolio grows without..." |

**Audit application:** Read the first three words of every headline, subhead,
and bullet on the page. If those words are about the company ("Our," "We,"
"With our"), the line needs to be front-loaded with a reader-relevant term
(margin, NOI, portfolio, vacancy, headcount, turnover).

## 13. The "You" Technique (Writing Process)

Beyond measuring the you:we ratio, Wiebe describes a concrete drafting method:

1. Take any line of copy.
2. Rewrite it so it starts with "you" or "your."
3. Edit for flow — the final version doesn't need to literally start with
   "you," but the rewrite forces a perspective shift.

Example:
- Draft: "We provide operational audits that identify cost savings."
- "You" rewrite: "You'll see exactly where your per-unit costs are leaking
  — and how to stop it."

This is a process step for rewrites, not just a post-hoc measurement. Apply
it to every line before editing for final voice and rhythm.

## 14. Copy as UX

Every line of copy is a UX element. It either builds momentum toward the
page's goal, delivers value the reader didn't have before reading it, or
moves the reader closer to the CTA. If a line does none of these, it's
friction.

Test each line: **Does this build momentum, deliver value, or advance the
reader?** If not, cut it or rewrite it.

This is distinct from the "So What?" sweep (which checks relevance). Copy
as UX checks for forward motion. A line can be relevant but still stall
momentum — e.g., a feature list with no connecting tissue that makes the
reader work to understand why they should keep going.

**Audit application:** Read the page top to bottom and mark any point where
momentum stalls — where the reader has to supply their own motivation to
keep scrolling. These are UX friction points regardless of whether the
content itself is accurate or relevant.

## 15. F-Pattern Scannability

Eye-tracking research shows B2B readers scan in an F-pattern: across the
top, down the left side, with occasional horizontal passes. Effective copy
respects this by:

- Placing the primary message in the first line of the hero, not buried
  in a subhead.
- Using benefit-driven subheads as left-margin anchors (the reader will
  scan these to decide whether to read the body copy).
- Keeping the first sentence of each paragraph loaded with the section's
  key point — not with a transitional phrase.
- Using bullets and short paragraphs to create visual entry points for
  the horizontal scanning passes.

**Audit application:** Imagine the page with only the first two words of
each line visible. Does the F-pattern skeleton communicate the core value
proposition? If the visible words are "Our team," "We provide," "With years,"
the page fails F-pattern scannability.
